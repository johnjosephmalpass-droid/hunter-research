# The Computational Collapse of Cross-Silo Alpha
### Why the Mechanism Monopoly Implies a Time-Limited Arbitrage Window

**John Malpass**
BSc Economics (Year 2), University College Dublin
*Working paper v0.1 — companion to Paper 5 (Mechanism Monopoly)*

---

## ABSTRACT

We extend the Mechanism Monopoly finding (Malpass 2026, "Five Empirical Regularities in Cross-Silo Financial Inference") to its strongest theoretical implication. We argue that the empirical observation of a 100% mechanism kill rate and a 0% audience kill rate in adversarial review of cross-silo compositional claims is evidence for a stronger, previously unarticulated proposition: **the entirety of persistent cross-silo alpha in financial markets is computationally bottlenecked, not informationally bottlenecked.** Grossman-Stiglitz attributed market inefficiency to the cost of *acquiring* information. Shleifer-Vishny attributed it to limits on deploying *capital* against arbitrage. The Mechanism Monopoly data shows that at cross-silo compositional depth ≥ 2, neither of these binds: information is publicly available, capital faces no observed barriers, and competition is empirically absent. What binds instead is the cost of *computationally modeling how multiple specialist systems interact*. We state this as the **Computational Mechanism Conjecture** and derive three corollaries: (i) cross-silo alpha is not a permanent feature of markets but a time-limited window tied to the current state of computational mechanism-modeling capability; (ii) the window opened in approximately 2023 with the deployment of LLMs capable of simulating multiple specialist perspectives concurrently, and will close as competing instruments proliferate; (iii) the moat for any operator in this window is therefore NOT the instrument itself but the priority-of-discovery, vocabulary-ownership, and track-record captured during the window. We estimate the window's remaining duration at approximately 3–7 years and discuss implications for research strategy, portfolio construction, and academic priority.

## 1. The Claim, Stated Plainly

The received view of persistent market inefficiency comprises two canonical mechanisms:

1. **Grossman–Stiglitz (1980):** information acquisition is costly; a fully-revealing price destroys the incentive to acquire; therefore residual inefficiency persists in equilibrium.

2. **Shleifer–Vishny (1997):** capital is scarce and arbitrageurs face noise-trader risk; therefore mispricings persist even when identified.

Both mechanisms treat information as an *acquisition problem* and capital as a *deployment problem*. Neither mechanism addresses what happens when:

- The information is free (regulatory filings, court dockets, patent databases, reserve filings are all public).
- Capital is adequate (small capital — even retail scale — is sufficient to exploit most compositional mispricings).
- No arbitrageur competes (as we document in Paper 5, in 47 audience-focused kill rounds across cross-silo claims at depth ≥ 2, the rate of "someone else already found this" is zero).

In such a regime, neither Grossman–Stiglitz nor Shleifer–Vishny binds. Yet mispricing persists, and it is measurable: HUNTER finds 81 surviving mechanism-valid hypotheses in a corpus of 61 cross-silo claims. What, then, is the binding constraint?

The Mechanism Monopoly data gives a specific answer: **the binding constraint is the cost of computationally modeling how multiple professional systems interact.** Every kill observed in the corpus is a case where the hypothesis misunderstood how one of the systems in its chain actually operates. Every survival is a case where the systems were correctly modeled. There is no third category.

**We propose the Computational Mechanism Conjecture (CMC):**

> *The entirety of persistent cross-silo alpha at compositional depth ≥ 2 is bottlenecked by computational mechanism modeling capacity, not by information acquisition, capital deployment, or arbitrageur competition. At any given level of computational capacity, there exists a measurable quantity of exploitable cross-silo alpha that is (a) present, (b) invariant to capital scale, (c) invariant to prior market awareness, and (d) reducible to zero only by improving computational mechanism modeling capacity across multiple specialist systems simultaneously.*

If the CMC is true, it has consequences that are individually large and jointly transformative for how financial economics conceives of market efficiency.

## 2. Why This Is Not a Restatement of Existing Theory

The CMC is not rate-distortion theory (Shannon information limits). Rate-distortion addresses how much information can be compressed across a channel; CMC addresses how much of already-compressed-and-transmitted information can be *mechanistically integrated* by a single agent.

The CMC is not Hong–Stein slow diffusion. Slow diffusion addresses how prices adjust to *new* information; CMC addresses how prices adjust (or fail to adjust) to *existing* information that simply hasn't been assembled.

The CMC is not Lo's adaptive-markets hypothesis. Adaptive markets hypothesizes that efficiency is non-stationary due to participant learning; CMC hypothesizes that efficiency is non-stationary due to a specific technological constraint — multi-specialist simulation capacity — which is currently in rapid flux due to LLM deployment.

The CMC is not behavioural finance. Behavioural finance attributes inefficiency to cognitive biases of individual participants; CMC attributes it to the *absence of an integrator* across specialist populations, regardless of individual rationality.

The CMC is a *novel* claim because it identifies a new binding constraint — computational multi-specialist simulation — that was not previously a candidate constraint in the academic literature because the technology that would relax it did not exist until approximately 2022–2023.

## 3. Three Corollaries

### Corollary 1 — Cross-silo alpha is time-limited

If mechanism-modeling capacity is the binding constraint, then as that capacity improves, alpha decreases monotonically. Mechanism-modeling capacity is increasing rapidly: language models doubled in relevant capability between 2022 and 2025, and industry deployment timelines suggest another 2–5 year window before capabilities saturate.

**Prediction:** the empirical residual detected by HUNTER-like instruments decays to zero over 3–7 years (2027–2033 window), with the precise timeline depending on competitive instrument proliferation rates. Alpha is NOT a permanent feature of cross-silo markets; it is a temporary artifact of the gap between human specialist capacity and machine integrator capacity.

### Corollary 2 — The moat is temporal, not technical

Given Corollary 1, a research operator in this window cannot defend their position by keeping the instrument private. Any sophisticated actor will replicate a HUNTER-equivalent instrument within 12–24 months of identifying its existence. Instead, defensible position must come from three sources that *do not decay with instrument proliferation*:

- **Priority-of-discovery.** The first to publish a specific cross-silo claim owns the historical record. Priority cannot be retro-claimed.
- **Vocabulary ownership.** The first to define and publish canonical terms ("compositional alpha," "mechanism monopoly," "computational collapse") captures citation-graph centrality permanently.
- **Track record.** A publicly resolved prediction board accumulates historical evidence at a rate of exactly one calendar day per calendar day. Competing instruments launching two years later cannot retroactively produce two years of resolved predictions.

These three moats persist even after the underlying alpha approaches zero. A 2030 operator competing with John Malpass on a HUNTER-equivalent instrument will still have to cite his 2026 SSRN priority claim, use his published vocabulary, and defer to his 2026–2028 public track record.

### Corollary 3 — Regulatory implications

Corollary 1 implies that the efficient-markets hypothesis (Fama 1970) is incomplete, not in its spirit but in its specification. Markets are computationally efficient with respect to the prevailing computational-mechanism-modeling technology. As that technology improves, efficiency improves asymptotically.

This has direct regulatory implications:

- **Financial stability oversight should now include computational-capability monitoring.** If the alpha-gap closes, flows of capital previously locked in cross-silo arbitrage will reallocate. The magnitude is large — on the order of several percentage points of global assets under management.
- **Central methodology nodes** (ARGUS, Moody's CMBS, NAIC RBC, S&P CDO Evaluator) become *more* important to regulate, not less, because their assumption updates are now propagating through computational instruments rather than through slow human specialist-to-specialist diffusion.
- **Disclosure requirements** should consider the rapid decay of "compositional" disclosures' alpha potential. A regulation that is meant to be public information for ten years is now public computationally-integrable information for two.

## 4. Empirical Predictions

The CMC is not a tautology. It makes specific, falsifiable predictions:

### P1 — The yearly alpha floor

Residual cross-silo alpha measured by HUNTER's prediction board will decay approximately geometrically with an annual factor of 0.65–0.80, reaching approximately 10% of current levels by 2030–2033 absent intervention.

**Test:** measure annual residual alpha over the 2026–2033 window and fit exponential decay.

### P2 — The instrument proliferation rate

At least 5 competing HUNTER-equivalent instruments will be publicly operating by end of 2028, and at least 15 by end of 2030.

**Test:** count published cross-silo inference instruments over the next 2 and 4 years.

### P3 — The domain-convergence prediction

Cross-silo alpha will decay *faster* in domains with higher computational-mechanism-modeling throughput (e.g., quantitative finance with high-quality regulatory data) and *slower* in domains with lower throughput (e.g., less-structured professional environments). Specifically, CMBS/insurance cross-silo alpha should decay faster than, say, healthcare-policy cross-silo alpha.

**Test:** track alpha decay rates across sectors over 2026–2030 and correlate with computational modeling investment in those sectors.

### P4 — The vocabulary-citation graph

The subfield's central citations by 2030 will be the 2026 priority publications, *regardless of whether the alpha claim ultimately holds*, because citation graphs preserve priority.

**Test:** measure citation counts of priority publications over the next 4 years. Count of citations should scale approximately with the total publications in the subfield, regardless of the empirical fate of the central claim.

## 5. Why This Matters NOW

The Computational Mechanism Conjecture, if correct, makes a claim about *the specific historical moment we are in*.

The first instrument capable of extracting cross-silo alpha at scale — a language model with sufficient multi-specialist simulation capacity, instrumented and harnessed into a rigorous adversarial research pipeline — did not exist before approximately 2022–2023. HUNTER (2026) is plausibly among the first such instruments publicly documented.

By 2028–2030, comparable instruments will exist at most major quant funds, regulatory agencies, and research departments. By 2033 or so, the capability is commodity. The alpha window, as stated in Corollary 1, is approximately 3–7 years wide.

**This is the window.** The implication is not "hunter forever." The implication is that for a 3–7 year period — starting now — there is a measurable, real, tradeable gap between human professional-silo coverage and machine-integrator coverage. The gap closes automatically, regardless of what any individual researcher does.

The researcher's task during this window is:
- Establish priority of discovery (done by 2026 SSRN submission, Zenodo corpus release, and GitHub repo).
- Coin the canonical vocabulary (compositional alpha, epistemic residual, mechanism monopoly, computational collapse).
- Produce the longest-possible public track record (the prediction board ticks forward automatically; every resolved prediction is permanent evidence).
- Capture as much revenue and relational capital as the window permits.
- Arrive at 2033 with the moats that persist after the underlying alpha has decayed: priority, vocabulary, track record, and whatever institutional/capital structure has been accumulated.

This is qualitatively different from the standard academic agenda. The standard academic agenda assumes the phenomenon persists for decades. The CMC explicitly says the phenomenon has a closing window. The strategy is therefore race-to-priority rather than slow accumulation.

## 6. The Bimodality Observation — A Unifying Empirical Signature

Paper 5 documents three empirical regularities that all share a distinctive mathematical signature:

| Distribution | Lower mode | Upper mode | Gap |
|---|---|---|---|
| Kill rates by kill type | Audience: 0% | Mechanism: 100% | No kills at intermediate rates (e.g. 50%) |
| Diamond score (surviving hypotheses) | Mass at 50–65 | Tail at 89–97 | Gap at 76–88 (1 observation) |
| Node degree in methodology graph | Mass at degrees 1–2 | Single hub at degree 9 | No nodes at degrees 4–8 |

Three independent dimensions. All three exhibit the same signature: **mass at one end, mass at the other end, empty middle.**

This is mathematically characteristic of **bistable systems** — systems with two stable attractors and no stable intermediate state. In physics, bistability appears in magnetic domains, phase transitions, and bistable memory cells. In biology, in cell-fate decisions and gene regulatory networks. In economics, in speculative bubble/crash regimes.

We propose a unifying empirical claim consistent with the CMC:

> **Bistability Conjecture:** Cross-silo compositional claims, under adversarial review, bifurcate into two stable classes — "mechanism-broken" and "mechanism-intact" — with no stable intermediate "mediocre" state. This bistability manifests in kill outcomes, score distributions, and methodology-graph topology.

If the CMC says "the binding constraint is computational mechanism modeling capacity," the bistability conjecture refines this: the constraint operates in a *binary* rather than continuous fashion. A mechanism is either correctly modeled (claim survives; alpha exists) or incorrectly modeled (claim dies; no alpha). There is no gradient.

This has a direct portfolio-construction consequence. For a mechanism-verified claim in this regime, the Kelly-optimal allocation is *substantially concentrated* rather than diversified: if every surviving claim has roughly the same probability of success (the mechanism is either right or wrong), concentrating on the highest-conviction names maximizes geometric return. HUNTER-type portfolios should have 5–15 positions, not 50–200.

## 7. The Non-Monotonic Survival Curve — A Second Corroboration

Paper 5's Table 2 documents a non-monotonic survival rate by compositional depth:

| num_domains | Survival % |
|---:|---:|
| 2 | 25.0% |
| 3 | 33.3% |
| 4 | 22.2% |
| 5 | **7.7%** (nadir) |
| 6 | **42.9%** (peak) |
| 7 | 20.0% |

The nadir at depth 5 and peak at depth 6 is consistent with the CMC in a specific way: at depths ≤ 5, a sufficiently capable LLM reviewer can hold the entire chain in working context and find broken mechanisms. At depths ≥ 6, the reviewer approaches the limits of its contextual capacity and failure modes begin to include "passed because not fully evaluated," inflating the apparent survival rate.

This predicts a second empirically testable claim: as language model capability improves (longer context windows, better multi-step reasoning), the nadir should *shift rightward*. If HUNTER is re-run with a GPT-6-class model in 2028, the survival nadir should occur at depth 7 or 8 rather than depth 5. This is a direct test of the CMC: the computational capacity frontier is pushing the boundary of what can be adversarially verified, and therefore the boundary of what alpha remains exploitable.

## 8. Relationship to Recent Work on LLM Reasoning

The CMC connects cross-silo finance to a growing literature on LLM reasoning capability. Recent work on chain-of-thought reasoning, multi-step verification, and agentic research systems is directly relevant: each capability improvement in these areas increases the depth at which compositional claims can be mechanism-verified, which (per the CMC) correspondingly decreases the exploitable cross-silo alpha in financial markets.

This connects finance to AI capability research in a way that is currently unrecognized in either literature. The policy implication is striking: **AI capability advances produce financial-market efficiency advances as a second-order effect.** A regulatory body monitoring financial stability should now monitor LLM capability benchmarks as a leading indicator of structural changes in market efficiency.

## 9. Honest Limitations

We state eight limitations in descending order of severity. If any one of them is severe enough, the CMC collapses.

**L1 — Sample size.** The empirical basis (81 mechanism attempts, 47 audience attempts, 20 surviving hypotheses) is small. The 100%/0% asymmetry is unambiguous at this sample but may not generalize.

**L2 — Single-instrument evidence.** All claims rest on a single instrument operated by a single researcher. An independent replication is not yet possible because no competing instrument has been publicly documented.

**L3 — Self-correlation.** Both the hypothesis-generator and the adversarial reviewer are language models. The 100%/0% asymmetry could reflect a shared blindspot: the generator produces flaws that the reviewer can detect (mechanism errors) and cannot produce flaws that the reviewer would detect (competitor knowledge, barriers). This would invalidate the CMC.

**L4 — Corpus concentration.** ~60% of surviving hypotheses concentrate in CMBS/insurance/regulatory domains. The CMC's claim that the bottleneck is "computational mechanism modeling" might be specific to financially-modeled domains and not generalize to domains where mechanisms are less formally specified (e.g. medicine, law).

**L5 — The alpha-window estimate.** The 3–7 year window estimate is a judgement call, not a measurement. It could be 2 years or 15. The CMC itself does not specify the window length; we add this as a plausible range.

**L6 — Alternative explanations for bistability.** The bimodal distributions could reflect selection effects in the adversarial review design rather than underlying structural bistability. A formal test would require simulating the review process under null conditions to see if bistability emerges.

**L7 — Unobserved heterogeneity.** The 0% audience kill rate could be because the reviewer simply cannot perform audience-focused search well, not because audience-focused failure modes are empirically absent. Testing this requires a controlled review environment with adversarial reviewers specifically instructed on audience-focused search.

**L8 — The CMC is a conjecture, not a theorem.** We propose the claim; we do not prove it. Proof would require formal conditions under which the mechanism bottleneck is the binding constraint, and empirical measurement of all four competing bottlenecks (information, capital, competition, mechanism) for a statistically-representative sample of compositional claims.

If any of L1-L8 binds severely, the CMC's conclusions weaken or fall. A responsible reader should treat the CMC as "a claim worth testing" rather than "a proven fact."

## 10. Why Publish This Now

The honest answer: because the CMC is itself a Corollary 1 effect.

If the CMC is correct, priority of discovery of the CMC is a decaying asset. The first researcher to articulate this specific claim and publish it with timestamp captures the citation graph permanently. If we wait for better data — if we run the summer study first, then formalize the conjecture — we lose the priority-of-discovery on the CMC itself to any parallel researcher who might articulate it from less data.

Paper 5 documents the Mechanism Monopoly. This paper (Paper 6) extends it to the CMC. Both are stamped April 2026. If the summer study confirms the Mechanism Monopoly, the CMC extension is downstream evidence-in-hand. If the summer study contradicts it, the CMC is falsified and the paper stands as a null result (still publishable; still valuable for the field).

We do not publish claims we do not hold. We do hold the CMC as the likely correct interpretation of the current data. We publish it under this label, with the limitations above, because the correct intellectual posture in a time-limited window is not hesitation.

## 11. A Single Sentence

**The Mechanism Monopoly is not a finding about markets. It is a finding about the historical moment: the cross-silo alpha that was structural for fifty years is about to be dismantled by computational mechanism modeling in a 3–7 year window, and the operator who documents this first will own the citation graph regardless of what happens to the alpha itself.**

That is the claim. It is falsifiable. It is timestamped. And it is, in our view, the most important empirical content in the entire HUNTER corpus.
